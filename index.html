Introduction

A Tool Without Judgment Is a Risk

Generative artificial intelligence has quickly become part of everyday life for software engineering students. It helps them write code, spot mistakes, and get through assignments faster than ever before. UNESCO recognizes that AI has real potential to improve how we teach and learn — but it also warns that technology is moving so fast that rules and guidance simply haven't kept up.

That gap matters a lot. The problem is not that AI tools exist, but that many students use them without really thinking about what they're doing or whether the results actually make sense. UNESCO's global survey found that fewer than 10% of schools and universities have any formal guidance on AI — which says a lot about why so many institutions are still catching up instead of leading the way.

This blog argues that software engineering education should respond with two connected strategies: teaching students to use AI responsibly, and making human review a non-negotiable part of every assignment.

Core thesis — Global Solvers Blog

That combination is stronger than banning AI or accepting it blindly, because it treats AI as something that helps you think better — not something that thinks for you.

Short Narrative · Tenses in Context

When Speed Beat Understanding

Past Simple Past Continuous Past Perfect Present Perfect

Daniel was completing his final software engineering project when he decided to use GitHub Copilot to speed up the authentication module. He had already fallen behind schedule because of other deadlines, so the tool seemed like the perfect shortcut. In just a few hours, he generated hundreds of lines of code and submitted the work with confidence.

Two weeks later, his professor was reviewing the submissions when she discovered a serious security flaw in Daniel's login system — the exact kind of mistake that had caused a real company's data breach that same semester. Daniel could not explain how the function was working or why the user data was not being protected properly. The code had been written for him, not by him.

Since that day, Daniel has learned that AI tools are powerful precisely because they are fast — and dangerous for exactly the same reason. That experience has changed the way he approaches every new project: he now reads, tests, and questions every line before he trusts it.

Step 4 · Problem Context

The Core Issue: Uncritical Dependence

The story above is fictional, but the risk is real. When students accept AI-generated code without checking it, they can easily miss mistakes, security flaws, or poor design choices they never even noticed. The result is a dangerous gap between looking productive and actually being competent.

GitHub's research shows that AI tools can make developers up to 55% faster — which is exactly what makes them so appealing, and exactly why they can become a crutch when students are not taught to think critically about what the AI is giving them.

The stakes go well beyond grades. Software is used in hospitals, banks, schools, and everywhere in between. If future engineers get used to trusting AI output without questioning it, they could enter the profession without the judgment they need to handle real problems — and real consequences.

Using AI Critically to Design the Solution

Building this blog involved using generative AI as a thinking tool — not something to copy from, but something to think alongside. Below is an honest account of the prompts used, what actually helped, what got thrown out, and which decisions stayed entirely human.

Prompts Used

1 Problem Exploration
"Now, we search solutions to the problem. All possible solutions — exhaustive analysis."
This moved the work from identifying the problem to actually exploring what could be done about it — and prevented settling on the first idea that came up.
2 Structural Evaluation
"Now, evaluate with this [SWOT template / rubric criteria]."
This connected the ideas to the actual evaluation criteria, so the comparison felt grounded in something concrete rather than just gut feeling.
3 Blog Drafting
"Turn this into a full blog draft of 700–1000 words in polished academic English."
Turned the raw ideas into a readable draft. That version was then reviewed, cut down, and rewritten based on what the rubric actually asked for.

Critical Evaluation of AI Responses

✓ What Was Useful

Having a clear structure for the problem statement — context, gap, significance — gave the whole blog a solid foundation to build on. The side-by-side comparison of solutions made it much easier to choose the two that actually made sense together. The rubric check was also genuinely helpful because it pointed out specific things that were missing, not just vague feedback.

✗ What Was Discarded

The first drafts were too broad and didn't have any real data behind them. Some versions ran way over 1000 words and started repeating themselves. Ideas like banning AI entirely or setting up external audit systems were also dropped — they sounded strong on paper but just weren't realistic for a university setting.

Human Decisions & Discipline Contribution

The decision to focus on software engineering specifically — rather than AI in education in general — came from the author, not from the AI. That choice made the whole proposal more concrete and honest: the risks described here are real ones that happen in this field, not abstract warnings. Choosing two solutions instead of one also came from that same engineering mindset — layering different checks is just how good software is built.

Ethical Reflection

Working this way made one thing very clear: AI is most useful when it helps you think better, not when it thinks for you. Every response the AI gave was checked against real sources, adjusted based on the rubric, and shaped by actual knowledge of the subject. There's something fitting about that — a blog about using AI responsibly, built by actually using AI responsibly.

Step 5 · Solution Overview

A Two-Part Response

The most honest response to this problem is a two-part strategy — one that neither bans AI nor pretends it has no risks.

Part 1: Teaching students how to use AI well. Universities should make it part of the curriculum to actually teach how generative AI works and how to use it without losing your own judgment. That means learning how to ask it the right questions, how to check the answers it gives, and how to recognize when something looks wrong. UNESCO's guidance on this is clear: AI in education should keep people at the center, and that starts with institutions building real policies — not just hoping students figure it out on their own. Students should not just submit code; they should be able to explain every decision they made and why.

Part 2: Making human review non-negotiable. Every piece of AI-generated work should go through a real check before it gets submitted. Not because AI is bad, but because that's just what responsible engineering looks like. GitHub's own data shows AI-assisted code can actually be quite good — but "quite good" still needs a human to make sure it does what it's supposed to do, that it holds up over time, and that it doesn't put anyone at risk.

Step 6 · Feasibility Plan

Implementation & SWOT Analysis

One of the strongest things about this proposal is that it doesn't require building something from scratch. It fits right into courses that already exist. A realistic first step would be adding a short module on responsible AI use at the beginning of a programming course, followed by grading criteria that ask students to show their work — not just the final result, but how they used AI and how they checked it.

The people involved would be students, professors, academic coordinators, and software engineering departments. What's needed isn't a huge budget — just clear teaching materials, some guidelines for reviewing code, and grading criteria that take the process seriously. And because this touches on engineering, ethics, education, and technology all at once, it's a genuinely interdisciplinary effort by design.

Strengths

  • Fits into existing courses without extra cost
  • Matches what the industry already expects from engineers
  • Builds habits that stay useful no matter how AI changes
  • Backed by UNESCO and GitHub research

Weaknesses

  • Professors need time and preparation to teach this well
  • Grading criteria might vary too much across courses
  • Some students may see it as extra work rather than real learning

Opportunities

  • Fills a real gap — less than 10% of universities have AI guidance
  • Can be adapted for other disciplines and countries
  • Helps graduates stand out as trustworthy AI users
  • Gives institutions a starting point for AI policy

Threats

  • AI tools evolve faster than curricula can keep up
  • Students might go through the motions without really learning
  • Adoption could be uneven across departments or schools
Steps 7 & 8 · Impact & Cultural Analysis

Long-Term Benefits & The Nosedive Warning

If this proposal were put into practice, the effects would show up quickly — and last a long time. Students would come out of their programs knowing how to catch their own mistakes, how to check their own work, and how to think through a problem step by step, rather than handing that process off to a machine. They would also be more honest about what AI can and can't do, which is a quality that matters a lot in professional settings where someone has to answer for what goes wrong.

On a larger scale, this approach gets students ready for a world where AI is already everywhere in the industry. The challenge isn't to push back against that — it's to make sure the people entering the field know how to handle it thoughtfully. A graduate who knows how to use AI well will be more adaptable, more reliable, and more valuable than one who just uses it fast.

Cultural Analysis Black Mirror — "Nosedive" (S03E01, Netflix)

Key Scene There's a moment in the episode where Lacie is stranded at an airport, desperately smiling and laughing with strangers she has nothing in common with — all to try to raise her social score enough to get on a flight. She's not being herself at all. Every move she makes is calculated around what the system will reward, not what she actually thinks or feels.

That scene is a little too close to what can happen academically. When students focus on getting the assignment done and getting a good grade — rather than actually understanding the material — they end up in the same trap as Lacie. They perform competence instead of building it. The score becomes the goal, and the real skill quietly disappears. The episode is exaggerated, obviously, but the warning is real: when any system rewards surface results too strongly, people start optimizing for the surface. Teaching students to review and question AI output is, in a way, teaching them to stay in control of their own judgment — not hand it over to something else.

Conclusion

Preparation, Not Prohibition

AI is changing what it means to study and work in software engineering — and that's not going to stop. But the real challenge isn't the technology itself. It's whether students learn to work with it honestly, or whether they learn to hide behind it. One path leads to engineers who can think on their own. The other leads to engineers who can't explain their own code.

In the future, the most valuable software engineers will not be the ones who use AI the fastest, but the ones who can still think clearly, question intelligently, and take responsibility for what they build.

The answer is not to ban AI — it's to take it seriously. Teaching students how to use it well, and making human review part of every workflow, is a realistic and responsible way forward. The goal is simple: when the next Daniel submits his project, he should actually understand every line of it.
